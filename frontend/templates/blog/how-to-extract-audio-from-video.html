How to Extract Audio from a Video File

May 5, 2024 5 min read

Whether you want to rip the soundtrack from a film, save a conference presentation as a podcast, extract music from a music video, or grab a voice recording from a video call — extracting audio from video is a common task. This guide explains how it works, which format to save the audio in, and how to do it in seconds without installing any software.

How Does Audio Extraction Work?

A video file is a container. Formats like MP4, MKV, MOV, and AVI each hold one or more streams of data: a video stream (the frames), one or more audio streams (the sound), and sometimes subtitles or chapter data. Extracting audio means reading the audio stream and saving it as a standalone audio file — the video stream is simply discarded.

There are two approaches:

  • Stream copy (lossless extraction): The audio data is copied out of the video container exactly as it is, with no re-encoding. This is instant and preserves the original audio quality perfectly. It only works if the audio codec in the video is already in a format you want (e.g. AAC inside an MP4 can be copied directly to an M4A file).
  • Re-encoding: The audio is decoded and then re-encoded into a new format. This takes slightly longer and involves a small quality loss (since the audio is being re-encoded), but gives you full control over the output format and quality. Required when converting to a different codec (e.g. extracting AAC audio from a video and saving it as MP3 or FLAC).

Which Output Format Should You Choose?

The right format depends on what you plan to do with the audio:

  • MP3 — Use when you need universal compatibility. Plays on every device, app, and platform. Choose 192 kbps or higher for good quality.
  • AAC / M4A — Better quality than MP3 at the same file size. The native format for Apple devices and the audio codec inside MP4 video. Good choice for podcast or streaming distribution.
  • WAV — Uncompressed, lossless. Large files but perfect quality. Use when you plan to edit the audio further in a DAW (Digital Audio Workstation).
  • FLAC — Lossless compression. Same quality as WAV but smaller files (roughly half the size). Ideal for archiving or critical listening. Not supported everywhere.
  • OGG — Open-source, royalty-free, comparable quality to AAC. Good for web applications and games.

Common Use Cases

Extracting music from a music video

Choose MP3 at 320 kbps or AAC at 256 kbps for good quality. If the video source is already compressed, re-encoding at a high bitrate will not recover quality that was lost in the original video compression — but it will preserve all the quality that remains.

Saving a lecture, webinar, or meeting recording as a podcast

Choose MP3 at 128 kbps or AAC at 96–128 kbps. Speech audio does not require high bitrates. After extracting, consider normalizing the loudness to −16 LUFS for consistent podcast playback.

Extracting audio for editing in a DAW

Choose WAV (uncompressed) or FLAC (lossless) to preserve full quality for editing. Avoid MP3 or AAC if you plan to process the audio further — decoding and re-encoding adds generation loss.

Creating an audio version of a video essay or documentary

Choose MP3 at 192 kbps for good quality and broad compatibility. Consider stripping any video-only elements and normalizing loudness before distributing.

What If the Video Has Multiple Audio Tracks?

Some video files — particularly MKV containers — contain multiple audio tracks (e.g. the original language track and a dubbed version, or a 5.1 surround track and a stereo track). PDFWorks extracts the first audio track by default. If you need to extract a specific track from a multi-track file, a desktop application like VLC or Handbrake gives you track selection control.

Will Extracted Audio Sound Different from the Video?

The extracted audio is identical to what you hear in the video — it is the same data. You will not gain or lose quality from the extraction itself (assuming you use the same codec, or a lossless target format). Any quality limitations in the audio were introduced when the video was originally encoded.
