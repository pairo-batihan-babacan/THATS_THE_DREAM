JPEG vs PNG vs WebP: Which Image Format Should You Use?

May 15, 2024 7 min read

JPEG, PNG, and WebP are the three formats you encounter on almost every website and in almost every workflow. They are not interchangeable — each was designed for a different purpose, and choosing the wrong format for the job creates unnecessarily large files or noticeable quality problems. This guide gives you the clear answer for every common situation.

The Short Answer

  • Photos and complex images: WebP (first choice) or JPEG (universal fallback)
  • Logos, icons, screenshots, text-heavy images: PNG
  • Images with transparency: WebP or PNG (JPEG cannot do transparency)
  • Maximum compatibility everywhere: JPEG (photos) or PNG (graphics)
  • Web performance: WebP for everything

JPEG (Joint Photographic Experts Group)

JPEG was standardized in 1992 and remains the most widely used image format in the world. It is a lossy format designed specifically for photographic images.

How JPEG compression works

JPEG divides the image into 8×8 pixel blocks and uses a Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) to represent each block's colour and brightness in frequency components. High-frequency detail (sharp edges, fine textures) is discarded more aggressively than low-frequency information (broad colour gradients). The quality setting (typically 0–100) controls how aggressively this discarding occurs.

JPEG strengths

  • Excellent compression for photographs — a 5 MB photo at quality 80 compresses to 200–500 KB
  • Universal support — every device, browser, and application handles JPEG
  • Adjustable quality/size trade-off

JPEG weaknesses

  • No transparency — JPEG does not support an alpha channel. All pixels are opaque.
  • Lossy artefacts on sharp edges — The block-based compression creates "mosquito noise" around sharp edges and text, making JPEG unsuitable for logos, screenshots, and diagrams.
  • Generation loss — Every time you save a JPEG, quality is lost. Never edit and re-save a JPEG repeatedly; keep an original in a lossless format.
  • Does not handle solid flat colours well

JPEG quality settings

  • Quality 90–100: Near-lossless. Large files. Use for archiving or printing.
  • Quality 75–85: High quality, good compression. The right range for web photos.
  • Quality 60–75: Visible artefacts on close inspection but good for thumbnails and previews.
  • Below 60: Noticeably degraded. Only appropriate for very small previews.

PNG (Portable Network Graphics)

PNG was created in 1996 as a patent-free replacement for GIF. It is a lossless format designed for graphics, screenshots, and images with transparency.

How PNG compression works

PNG uses DEFLATE compression (the same algorithm as ZIP files) applied after a filtering step that increases compressibility. It is entirely lossless — a PNG decoded and re-encoded is bit-for-bit identical to the original. No quality is ever lost.

PNG strengths

  • Lossless: Perfect pixel-for-pixel preservation of the original image
  • Full transparency support: 8-bit alpha channel for any level of opacity, including semi-transparent pixels
  • No artefacts on sharp edges: Ideal for logos, icons, text, screenshots, and any image with flat areas of colour
  • Universal support
  • Supports very high bit depths (up to 16-bit per channel)

PNG weaknesses

  • Large files for photographs: A PNG of a photograph is typically 5–10× larger than a JPEG at equivalent perceptual quality. Never use PNG for photographs on the web.
  • Slower to encode and decode than JPEG

When PNG file sizes surprise you

If you have a PNG of a photograph that is unexpectedly large, that is by design — it is losslessly preserving every pixel of that photograph. Convert it to JPEG or WebP for web use and the file will shrink dramatically with minimal perceptual quality loss.

WebP

WebP was developed by Google and released in 2010. It supports both lossy and lossless compression, transparency, and animation — and consistently produces smaller files than JPEG and PNG for equivalent quality.

WebP vs JPEG (lossy)

WebP lossy compression is based on the VP8 video codec's intraframe encoding. It uses larger block sizes, more sophisticated prediction, and better entropy coding than JPEG's DCT approach. The result: WebP lossy files are typically 25–35% smaller than JPEG at equivalent visual quality, or equivalently, better quality at the same file size.

WebP vs PNG (lossless)

WebP lossless uses a different algorithm from lossy WebP. It is typically 26% smaller than PNG for the same image content.

WebP strengths

  • Smaller files than both JPEG and PNG at equivalent quality
  • Supports transparency (unlike JPEG)
  • Supports animation (like GIF, but more efficient)
  • Both lossy and lossless modes in one format
  • Supported by all modern browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari 14+, Edge)

WebP weaknesses

  • Not supported in older browsers (Internet Explorer, Safari before 2020)
  • Not universally supported in image editors — some older tools cannot open or export WebP
  • Not ideal for print workflows; JPEG or TIFF are better for print
  • The lossy/lossless distinction is not always obvious from the file — you cannot tell by looking at the extension

The Simple Decision Guide

  • Photo for a website: WebP (with JPEG fallback if you need to support older browsers)
  • Logo or icon with transparency: WebP or PNG
  • Screenshot of an app or website: PNG (preserves text sharpness) or WebP lossless
  • Photo to email to family: JPEG — universally supported, they will definitely be able to open it
  • Image to edit further: Keep in PNG or TIFF for editing; export to JPEG/WebP for distribution
  • Animation: WebP (smaller than GIF, supports true colour); or GIF if compatibility is critical
