What Is Audio Normalization? LUFS, EBU R128 and Loudness Explained

May 3, 2024 7 min read

You have probably experienced it: you are watching a playlist or listening to a podcast and every few minutes you reach for the volume knob because the next track is dramatically louder or quieter. Audio normalization is the process that fixes this — it adjusts a file's overall loudness to a consistent target level. This guide explains what normalization actually does, what LUFS means, what the EBU R128 standard is, and when you should (and shouldn't) normalize your audio.

What Is Audio Normalization?

Normalization is the process of adjusting the overall loudness of an audio file so that its loudness matches a target value. The goal is consistency: if you normalize multiple files to the same target, they will all play at approximately the same perceived volume without the listener needing to adjust the volume control between tracks.

There are two fundamentally different types of normalization:

Peak normalization

Peak normalization finds the loudest single sample in the file and scales the entire file so that sample hits a specific maximum level — typically 0 dBFS (decibels relative to full scale, the maximum before digital clipping). Peak normalization prevents clipping but does not reliably produce consistent perceived loudness, because a file with one very loud peak but mostly quiet content can still feel much quieter than a consistently loud file with the same peak level.

Loudness normalization (the modern standard)

Loudness normalization measures the integrated loudness of the entire file — accounting for how loud the programme material sounds over its whole duration, weighted by how sensitive human hearing is at different frequencies — and adjusts the file to hit a target integrated loudness level. This produces much more consistent perceived volume across different files. Loudness normalization is measured in LUFS.

What Is LUFS?

LUFS stands for Loudness Units relative to Full Scale. It is the unit used to measure integrated loudness in modern audio production. The measurement is defined by the ITU-R BS.1770 standard and accounts for:

  • K-weighting — a frequency response curve that mimics how the human ear perceives loudness at different frequencies (we are most sensitive to mid-range frequencies)
  • Gating — quiet sections (pauses, silence) are excluded from the measurement, so the loudness reflects the actual programme material, not the silences
  • Integration over time — rather than measuring a single moment, LUFS measures the average over the full duration

LUFS values are negative numbers. A quieter file has a more negative LUFS value. A louder file has a less negative (closer to 0) LUFS value. Typical ranges:

  • −6 to −9 LUFS — very loud (over-compressed commercial music)
  • −14 LUFS — streaming platform standard (Spotify, YouTube, Apple Music)
  • −16 LUFS — podcasting and general online content
  • −23 LUFS — broadcast standard (EBU R128)
  • −30 LUFS and below — very quiet material (classical music with wide dynamic range)

What Is EBU R128?

EBU R128 is the European Broadcasting Union's recommendation for loudness normalisation and permitted maximum level for programmes. Published in 2010, it established −23 LUFS as the target integrated loudness for broadcast audio in Europe and became the basis for loudness standards around the world.

The key specifications in EBU R128 are:

  • Integrated loudness target: −23 LUFS (also written −23 LU)
  • True peak maximum: −1 dBTP (to prevent inter-sample clipping during encoding)
  • Loudness Range (LRA): a measure of dynamic variation — how much the loudness varies throughout the programme

The broadcast standard of −23 LUFS is considerably quieter than streaming content. This is intentional: broadcast content is mixed with wide dynamic range and is consumed in environments (living rooms, offices) where the listener adjusts volume once and leaves it. Streaming content is often consumed on headphones in noisy environments and benefits from a louder, more consistent presentation.

Streaming Platform Loudness Targets

All major streaming platforms now apply automatic loudness normalization. If your upload is louder than their target, they turn it down. If it is quieter, some platforms leave it as-is (or turn it up slightly). The current targets are:

  • Spotify: −14 LUFS integrated (turns down if louder, leaves alone if quieter)
  • Apple Music: −16 LUFS (Sound Check feature)
  • YouTube: −14 LUFS integrated
  • Tidal: −14 LUFS
  • Amazon Music: −14 LUFS

This means there is no point in making your music as loud as possible before uploading — Spotify will turn it down anyway. Optimizing for streaming means targeting −14 LUFS and ensuring your true peak is at or below −1 dBTP to prevent clipping during their codec encoding.

When Should You Normalize Audio?

Normalize when:

  • You have multiple recordings made at different times or with different equipment and you want them to sound consistent when played sequentially
  • You are preparing a podcast episode — podcasts should target −16 LUFS for good intelligibility across earbuds, car speakers, and laptop speakers
  • You are uploading music, video, or a spoken-word recording to a streaming platform
  • You recorded something too quietly and want to bring it up to a usable level without re-recording
  • You are producing content for broadcast and need to meet EBU R128 or similar regulatory standards

Do not normalize when:

  • You are working on a project that still needs mixing and mastering — normalize at the final export stage, not mid-workflow
  • The file is an archival recording you want to preserve exactly as recorded
  • The loudness variation is intentional (e.g. a field recording of an environment, or a classical piece with significant dynamic range that is part of the artistic intent)

How Loudness Normalization Works Technically

The standard loudness normalization pipeline follows these steps:

  1. Analysis pass: The audio is analysed from start to finish to compute the integrated LUFS value of the whole file.
  2. Gain calculation: The difference between the measured LUFS and the target LUFS is calculated. For example, if the file measures −20 LUFS and the target is −16 LUFS, the gain needed is +4 LU.
  3. Gain application: The gain is applied uniformly to the entire file.
  4. True peak limiting: The output is checked to ensure no samples exceed the maximum true peak level (usually −1 dBTP). If applying the gain would cause clipping, a limiter is applied.
