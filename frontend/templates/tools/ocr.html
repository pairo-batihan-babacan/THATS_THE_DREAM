What Is OCR?

OCR stands for Optical Character Recognition. It is the technology that reads text from images — scanned documents, photographs of pages, screenshots, and any image where text appears visually — and converts it into machine-readable, selectable, copyable text.

Without OCR, a scanned PDF or photo is just an image. A computer cannot search it, copy text from it, or process it. OCR analyses the shapes of letters and words in the image and outputs the text content, making it editable and searchable.

What Types of Images Work Best?

OCR accuracy depends heavily on image quality. For best results:

  • High resolution: Scans at 300 DPI or higher produce the most accurate results. Camera photos should be taken in good lighting, in focus, and as straight-on to the page as possible.
  • High contrast: Black text on white background is ideal. Coloured backgrounds, low contrast, or faded ink reduce accuracy.
  • Clean fonts: Standard printed text (Times New Roman, Arial, standard body fonts) is recognised very accurately. Highly stylised, handwritten, or decorative text is harder to process.
  • Flat and unwarped: Pages photographed at an angle or with significant curvature (as with open books) introduce errors. Flatten the page before photographing where possible.

  • BMP — Windows bitmap format

Tips for Better OCR Results

  • Scan at 300 DPI minimum. Most modern flatbed scanners default to 200 DPI — change this to 300 or 600 for text extraction.
  • Use greyscale or black and white mode for text-only documents. Colour scanning adds file size without improving OCR accuracy on printed text.
  • Correct skew before uploading. If the page is rotated more than a few degrees in the image, OCR accuracy drops. Many scanner apps and photo editors include a straighten/deskew feature.
  • Remove noise. Speckled, noisy images from old or dirty scanners increase error rates. Use the scanner's cleanup settings or a tool like GIMP to reduce noise before OCR.

What About Handwriting?

Our OCR engine is optimised for printed, typed text — not handwriting. Handwritten text can sometimes be partially recognised if the handwriting is very clear and printed (not cursive), but results are unreliable and should be treated as a starting point requiring significant manual correction rather than a final output.
